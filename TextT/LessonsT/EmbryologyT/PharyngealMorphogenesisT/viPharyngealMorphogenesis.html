Pharyngeal Morphogenesis


Pharynx
Pharyngeal morphogenesis begins with widening of the cranial end of the gut.
Pharyngeal arches develop by migration of neural crest cells into the ventral sides of the embryonic head.
Neural crest mesenchyme condenses to form cartilaginous cores in the arches.
A pharyngeal artery and muscle also form in each arch.
Four additional arches will form, caudal to the first.
Pharyngeal Arches
There is no 5th arch in humans. It either fails to form, or a rudiment forms and regresses.
The first arch bifurcates into two processes known as the maxillary and mandibular prominences.
Arteries, pouches and cores, along with the nerves and muscles, are arranged like parallel cords running through each arch.
Pharyngeal arches are bulges on the lateral sides of the embryonic head.
Pharyngeal grooves are furrows between the arches.
Pharyngeal pouches are internal furrows, adjacent to each groove.
Pharyngeal membranes are the tissue between the grooves and pouches.
The first pharyngeal groove contributes to the external ear canal, the others regress.
The pouches also contribute to the internal ear, as well tonsils, thymus and parathyroid gland.
The arches produce skeletal components including facial and hyoid bones, laryngeal cartilages, and muscles for mastication and facial expression.
The 2nd and 6th arches expand over the 3rd and 4th arches. Thus arches 3 and 4 are internalized. The resultant cervical sinus will eventually vanish.
The cartilaginous cores of the 4th and 6th arches will fuse to form the larynx.
Facial Morphogenesis
The stomodeum develops into the mouth.
The otic pit develops into the inner ear.
The lens placode develops into the lens and cornea.
The nasal placode develops into nostrils.
The maxillary prominence develops into the upper jaw.
The mandibular prominence forms the lower jaw.
Pharyngeal arch 2 contributes to the hyoid.
As brain vesicles develop into the brain, their orientation shifts from perpendicular to the body axis, to in line and superior to the axis. As a result, facial structures shift from underneath the brain vesicles to a rostral position.
The mandibular and maxillary prominences on each side of the head expand medially and fuse, forming the maxilla and mandible.
Six auricular hillocks bud from the mandibular and maxillary prominences. These will develop into external ears.
The embryo assumes a humanoid appearance early in development, about week 8.
Further development into the fetus entails enlargement of structures, remodeling their shape, and advancing the complexity of tissues.
