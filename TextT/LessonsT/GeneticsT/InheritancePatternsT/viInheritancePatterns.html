Genetic Variability
Genetic Variability is Differences in Degree of the Phenotype Produced by the Same Genotype
Evaluated by Familial Studies Correlating Differences with Relationship, Twin Studies
Example given, Osteogenesis Imperfecta, Brittle Bone Disease
Severity Can Differ Amongst Relatives with the Same Mutation
Some Able to Withstand Greater Forces, Some Have More Fractures
Brittle bones are the distinguishing symptom of OI.
Blue sclerae occurs with most types of OI.
Skeletal deformities also occur with most types. The severity of these deformities varies with the type and individual case.
Other symptoms that commonly occur with different types of OI include short stature, dentinogenetic imperfecta, hearing loss, loose joints, poor muscle tone and respiratory problems.
Penetrance
Incomplete or Partial Penetrance is a mutation that does not produce the mutant phenotype in everyone who inherits the mutant genotype
Note this is not variability. The question is whether the patient demonstrates any symptoms or not, all or none, not the number or severity of symptoms.
Penetrance, the fraction of individuals with a mutant genotype who manifest the mutant phenotype If only 30% of homozygotes for a recessive mutation are affected, then the penetrance is 30%.
If all homozygotes are affected, the penetrance is 100%.
A Penetrant is a mutated individual who demonstrates the phenotype
Examples, BRCA 1 and BRCA 2 mutations that increase the risk of breast cancer
Both are Autosomal Dominant, Tumor Suppressors involved with DNA Repair
Risks of women developing breast cancer with BRCA1 is about 85% by age 65 years
With BRAC2 the lifetime risk is about 35%
Thus 15% of women with a BRCA1 mutation, and 65% with a BRCA2 mutation, never develop breast cancer.
Although BRCA mutations do increase the risk of breast cancer, neither is required.
95% of women with breast cancer do not have a BRCA mutation.
Other Risks with BRCA Mutations
Ovarian (≈40% for BRCA1), Bowel and Prostate Cancers
The risk of Males developing Breast Cancer with BRCA2 is about 6%. There is no increased risk for males with BRCA1.

Multifactorial Traits, phenotypes determined by multiple factors, both genetic and environmental
Polygenic Traits, phenotypes determined by multiple genes

Genetic Heterogeneity
Genetic Heterogeneity (Genocopy) , Similar Phenotypes Produced by Different Mutations
Genetic heterogeneity is subdivided into allelic and locus heterogeneity.
Allelic Heterogeneity , different mutations in the same gene producing similar phenotypes
Locus Heterogeneity , mutations in different genes producing similar phenotypes

Mitochondrial Inheritance
Maternal Inheritance, transmission from mothers to all children regardless of sex, never transmitted from fathers to any children.
Heteroplasmy , mixed intracellular populations of mitochondria with mutant and wild type genomes.
Because the proportions of mutant and wild type mitochondria can change, symptoms can vary from generation to generation, or even during the lifetime of an individual.
Example 1, Leber Hereditary Optic Neuropathy
Symptoms, Optic Atrophy Leading to Blindness, Telangiectatic Microangiopathy (small spider like capillaries)
Point mutations of components of NADH dehydrogenase complex I subunits (ND1, ND4, ND4L & ND6), blocking oxidative phosphorylation and ATP synthesis.
Example 2, Kearns Sayre Syndrome
Symptoms, Ophthalmoplegia (paralysis of eye muscles), Ptosis (difficulty opening eye), Pigmentary Retinopathy, Hypoparathyroidism, Cardiac Conduction Abnormalities, Diabetes, Deafness
Different genes lost with different microdeletions of mitochondrial DNA lead to this disease, including components of oxidative phosphorylation
Anticipation
A mutant phenotype that increases in severity each generation
Example, Fragile X Syndrome
Grandmother, No Symptoms, Daughter, Moderate Symptoms, Grandson, Severe Symptoms
Trinucleotide Repeat Expansion (FMR1)
Anticipation caused by Trinucleotide Repeat Expansion
C G G trinucleotide repeats are located in the 5' U T R of the wild type F M R 1 gene.
A grandparent will have the normal number of trinucleotide repeats, 6 to 54.
A parent in the next generation inherits 55 to 200 repeats.
This is known as a Premutation
This parent will have moderate symptoms.
This expansion occurs during oogenesis in the parent’s ovaries.
The number of repeats in the grandchild will increases even further, to over 200.
These repeats attract methylation.
The promoter, which is adjacent to the repeats, becomes methylated along with the repeats.
This prevents expression of the FMR1 gene.
The gene product of the FMR1 gene is FMRP
FMRP is a Chaperone Protein for Cerebral mRNAs
Lack of FMRP results in fragile X syndrome.
Anticipation is produced by trinucleotide repeat expansion.
Fragile X
Fragile X Chromosome
The Xq27.3 site shows a constriction that fails to stain.
The constriction is produced by the expansion of the trinucleotide repeats. This region is prone to breakage in tissue culture cells grown in the absence of folic acid. This fragility is an in vitro phenomenon. It does not occur in a patient's cells.
Inheritance pattern, X Linked Dominant, Partial Penetrance, about 55% in females, anticipation Symptoms
Moderate IQ, Most common heritable intellectual disability, second only to Down's
Hyperactivity, Hand flapping, Biting, Temper tantrums, Poor eye contact
After Puberty, Long face, Prominent jaw and forehead, Large ears, Macro orchidism (large testicles)

Epigenetic Imprinting
A mutant genotype with a different phenotype depending on context, for instance the sex of the parent
Imprinting refers to genomic reprograming, typically by methylation. This is the basis of epigenetics.

Microdeletion
FISH can be used to diagnose Angelman’s Syndrome
A locus specific probe for 15q12 is used.
This is the region responsible for Angelman’s.
If both homologs are marked by the Angelman’s probe, the patient does not have Angelman’s.
If only one homolog is marked, the 15q12region is deleted, confirming that the patient has Angelman’s syndrome.
The informative result was that when chromosomes from a Prader Willi patient was probed with the 15q12 fragment, only one homolog was marked.
Lack of Angelman’s signal in Prader Willi patients demonstrates that both syndromes are caused by the same microdeletion of the 15q12 region.
If the microdeletion is inherited from the mother, the child gets Angelman’s. The same microdeletion inherited from the father results in Prader Willi .
Methylation
A hallmark of imprinting is that genes are differentially methylated. Methylated genes are inactivated; non methylated genes are expressed.
The 15q12 region undergoes parental specific imprinting,
SNORD116 genes are methylated on the mother's homolog, active on the father's homolog.
UBE3A is the opposite, active on the mother's homolog, methylated on the father's homolog.
Additional genes are within the 15q12 region. OCA2 is an example that is not methylated.
When the microdeletion is inherited from the father, deletion of the SNORD116 complex from his homolog prevents expression of his allele. Methylation of the mother's homolog prevents expression of her allele. Thus, there's no expression of SNORD116. The result is Prader Willi syndrome.
The same deletion from the mother's homolog results in Angelman’s. In this case UBE3A is not expressed while the SNORD116 cluster is.
Note, OBA2 is expressed whether the microdeletion occurs with the father's or mother's homolog, although at reduced a level because it is only expressed from one allele. OBA2 induces melanin synthesis, and reduction of its expression is believed to be responsible for the hypopigmentation observed with Angelman’s and Prader Willi syndromes.
Epigenetics demonstrates that methylation patterns vary between male and female pronuclei, even though they appear identical.
