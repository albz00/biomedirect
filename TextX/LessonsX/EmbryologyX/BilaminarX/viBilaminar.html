The Bilaminar Stage


Implantation
During implantation, trophoblast cells adjacent to the underlying inner cell mass attach to the endometrial mucosa.
Syncytiotrophoblast
Trophoblast cells expel nuclei to form a syncytium surrounding the implanted blastocyst, known as the syncytiotrophoblast.
A syncytium consists of multiple nuclei in a common cytoplasm.
The remaining cells of the trophoblast become known as the cytotrophoblast.
Lamination
The ICM laminates into two layers, the epiblast and hypoblast.
The hypoblast assembles into a single layer of squamous cells.
Lateral epiblast cells migrate around the overlying cytotrophoblast, to form a new layer known as the amnion.
The amnion is occasionally called the extraembryonic ectoderm.
Epiblast cells that remain adjacent to the hypoblast assemble into a single layer of columnar cells.

At this stage the epiblast constitutes an oval sheet.
The epiblast will develop into the fetus.
A fluid filled space forms between the epiblast and amnion. This space is known as the amniotic cavity.
The fetus will grow into the amniotic cavity, which serves to cushion the fetus.
Amniocentesis is when a sample of amniotic fluid is extracted, although this procedure cannot be conducted until much later in gestation, 11 weeks at the earliest.
The epiblast and hypoblast constitute the bilaminar germ disc.
The bilaminar stage refers to this disc. Yolk Sac
The hypoblast expands around the periphery of the blastocoel to form the extraembryonic endoderm.
The extraembryonic endoderm eventually surrounds the blastocoel.
The space that used to constitute the blastocoel, along with the extraembryonic endoderm, is now known as the primary yolk sac.
Extraembryonic Mesoderm
Extraembryonic mesoderm differentiates from the extraembryonic endoderm.
The extraembryonic mesoderm spreads to create and fill a new space between the yolk sac and amnion, and the cytotrophoblast.
Lacunae Stage
Lacunae form in the extraembryonic mesoderm and syncytiotrophoblast.
A lacuna is a fluid filled space within a tissue.
This period is often referred to as the lacunar stage.
Trophoblastic lacunae are the beginning of placenta formation, which is explained in the Placenta lesson.
Chorion
The lacunae in the extraembryonic mesoderm expand and coalesce to form a new space known as the chorionic cavity, which splits the extraembryonic mesoderm.
Splanchnic extraembryonic mesoderm coats the yolk sac.
Somatic extraembryonic mesoderm coats the cytotrophoblast & amnion.
The chorion consists of three layers, syncytiotrophoblast, cytotrophoblast and somatic extraembryonic mesoderm.
The connecting stalk is a column of somatic extraembryonic mesoderm that tethers the amnion, yolk sac and germ disc, to the chorion. It will develop into the umbilical cord
Secondary Yolk Sac
A segment pinches off of the primary yolk sac. This is the exocoelomic cyst. The remainder is now known as the secondary yolk sac.
Normally, the structure is simply called a yolk sac.
It would only be called primary or secondary if it's necessary to distinguish one from the other.
Eventually the exocoelomic cyst regresses, leaving the secondary yolk sac.
