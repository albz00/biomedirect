This script is provided for use by the visually impaired. It is intended to be read by a text to speech translator. Many of the words and abbreviations are spelled phonically for correct pronunciation by the translator.

Orientations


Body Axes
Four axes are used to designate the spatial orientation of structures of the body.
Ventral means towards the belly.
Dorsal is towards the back.
Anterior is the front, in explanation, the forward direction of movement, the direction that the animal walks, swims or flies towards.
Because primates walk on two legs, they move in the direction of their belly, and their anterior is synonymous to ventral.
Most animals move towards their head, with their belly horizontal to the ground, so anterior is usually synonymous to cranial.
Posterior is the rear. In primates it is synonymous to dorsal; in most animals it is synonymous to caudal.
Lateral is towards the left or right sides of the body.
Medial is towards the midline.
Cranial is towards the head.
Caudal is towards, or below, the rump.
Superior is synonymous with cranial.
Rostral is towards the nose. During early embryogenesis this is synonymous with cranial.
Inferior is synonymous to caudal.
Proximal is towards to the base of a structure. For appendages this is towards the midline of the body.
Distal is towards to the end of a structure. For appendages this is away from the midline of the body.
Body Planes
Four hypothetical planes are used to define cross sections through the body.
A coronal plane divides the body into dorsal, ventral parts.
A transverse plane divides the body into caudal, cranial parts.
Transverse planes are also known as horizontal planes.
A sagittal plane divides the body into left, right parts.
Sagittal planes are also known as longitudinal planes.
The medial plane is the central sagittal plane, that divides the body along the midline.
