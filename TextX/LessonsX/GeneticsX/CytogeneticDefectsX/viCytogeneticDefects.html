Aneuploidies
Aneuploidies are when there is the wrong number of a specific chromosome, either trisomy or monosomy. These will be listed from the most to least common.
Triple X syndrome, genotype Trisomy X, XXX, incidence one in 500.
Extra Y syndrome, genotype XYY, incidence one in 500
Klinefelter’s syndrome, genotype XXY, incidence one in 500
Down’s syndrome, genotype trisomy 21, Incidence one in 800
Turner’s syndrome, genotype X monosomy, Incidence one in 4000
Edwards syndrome, genotype trisomy 18, Incidence one in 8000
Patau’s syndrome, genotype trisomy 13, Incidence one in 12000
Contrary to common belief the frequency of Down’s is the most frequent, although it is comparable to the extra sex chromosomes.
All chromosomes are believed to undergo nondisjunction at the same frequency.
The incidence of aneuploidies at birth is determined by spontaneous abortion rates.
Down’s
Trisomy 21 (47, XY, +21)
Origins of Down’s Syndrome
Trisomy 21, 94%, from Meiotic Nondisjunction
Mosaics, 3%, from Early Mitotic Nondisjunction
Partial Trisomy, 3%, from Translocations
The defect is inherited from the mother 80% to 95% of the time.
Critical Region
Partial trisomies demonstrated that patients with three copies of band 21q22 will have Down’s syndrome.
Three copies of other regions of chromosome 21 do not result in Down’s.
Thus, q22 is the critical region for Down’s syndrome.

Screens
Screening for Down’s in Pregnancy
Quad Screen, Maternal Serum with the following results is indicative of Down’s syndrome
Low alpha fetoprotein, high human chorionic gonadotropin, Low Estriol, high Inhibin
Fetal Nuchal Translucency Measurements
Absence of Fetal Nasal Bone
Amniocentesis, Cytogenetic Analysis, recommended Maternal Age, 35 years or older
Why 35?
After 35 year old the chance of detecting a cytogenetic defect exceeded the risk of miscarriage due to amniocentesis.
There is not a marked increase in risk of aneuploidy at maternal age 35; that is just a point on a continuously increasing curve.
35 years is simply the point where the risk of having a child with an aneuploidy exceeded the risk of miscarriage induced by amniocentesis.
Therefore, it is inappropriate to tell a patient that her risk will increase when she reaches 35 years; her risk is already increasing before she reaches that age.
The 35 year old criteria presume a judgmental value that many will not share; i.e. having a child with an aneuploidy is worse than losing a potentially healthy pregnancy.
Many parents have fulfilling relationships with their aneuploidy children, even if those children have severe symptoms, and many aneuploid patients live happy, enriching lives.
Therefore, for many parents, having a child with an aneuploidy may not be worse than losing a pregnancy.
Moreover, these criteria are outdated because the risk of miscarriage induced by amniocentesis has decreased due to improvements with the technology.
Due to considerations like these, genetic counseling should always be non directive. Counselors should provide parents and/or patients (if the patients are old enough) with all relevant information without interjecting their own values, and let those parents and/or patients make decisions that are right for them.
Edwards
Trisomy 18 (47, XX, +18)
Unlike Down’s, there is no critical region for Edwards.
Different partial trisomies along the length of chromosome 18 all produced symptoms of Edwards.
Symptoms
Clenched Fists, Overlapping Fingers (2nd over 3rd, 5th over 4th)
Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR)
Severe Intellectual Disability, no Psychosocial Development
Early Death, usually under one year old
Prominent Occiput
Micrognathia which is a small jaw
Posteriorly Rotated, Fawn Like Ears
Sloping Forehead
Microcephaly
Narrow Hips
Small Nails
Fingerpads with simple, Low Arch Patterns
Cardiac, genital and urinary deformities
The most distinguishing malformation of Edwards is the overlapping digits.
If this is observed the patient should be considered Edwards until proven otherwise.
Because of the lack of psychosocial development, the child will not be able to focus on or even recognize people.
It is important to prepare parents for the eventuality of little interaction with their child.
Narrow Hips
Small Nails
The clinched fists and overlapping digits occur because Edwards patients cannot open their hands.
Fawnlike ears are a common congenital defect. They occur because during embryogenesis ears fail to completely move from the posterior to lateral sides of the head.
Other common congenital defects include cleft lips and palate, clubbed and rocker bottom feet, microcephaly and microsomia, syndactyly and clinodactyly. These are not diagnostic for any specific syndrome, but they are suggestive of cytogenetic or teratogenic defects.
Mortality
Postnatal mortality is extremely high with Edwards, as is prenatal mortality.
Most Edwards newborns die within a week. However, a few can survive for years. The oldest known patient survived for over 34 years.
It is important to prepare parents for the probability of a brief period of time to spend with their Edwards child, as well as the hope for extended life with all the joy and hardship that will entail.
The incidence of females with Edwards is higher than males. This is because mortality is higher with males.
Growth rates are lower than normal with Edwards. Those few who survive to adulthood seldom reach more than 50 lbs.

Klinefelter’s Syndrome
47, XXY
Symptoms
Slightly Decreased IQ

Infertility (95%)

Klinefelter’s is underdiagnosed due to late onset of relatively mild symptoms.
The hallmark of Klinefelter’s is feminization.
The majority (70% to 85%) of patients with Klinefelter’s do not have gynecomastia. Moreover, many teenage boys develop gynecomastia even though they do not have Klinefelter’s. Therefore, although there is an increased incidence of gynecomastia with Klinefelter’s, it is not pathognomonic.
Small testicles are the most distinguishing symptom of Klinefelter’s.

Sex Determining Region of Y
The SRY gene is the critical region for male development. If an individual has this region he develops as a male. Without it she develops as a female.
Note, most XX males are phenotypically normal, while most XY females have Turner’s to like phenotypes.
Chromosomal Aberrations
Remember a chromosomal aberration is a structural mutation within a chromosome, deletion, duplication, insertion, inversion, translocation.
A very rare example is chromosome 3 duplication deletion syndrome, a very rare cytogenetic disorder resulting from an inversion within chromosome 3.
