Sexual Genetics


Glossary
Sex Determining Chromosomes, X and Y
Autosome, any chromosome except X or Y
X Linked, a gene located on the X chromosome
Note, X Linked genes are often referred to as "sex X Linked." However, this is imprecise because there are a few genes on the Y chromosome, which also qualify as sex X Linked.
Autosomal, any gene not located on X or Y, or characteristic not associated with X or Y
Hemizygote, a male with half as many X Linked alleles
There are few genes on the Y chromosome. So, males only have one allele for most X linked genes. This is half the number in females, and half the number for autosomal genes, hence the prefix hemi, which means half.
Sex Influenced Expression, autosomal traits, differentially expressed in the sexes example given body hair and aggression
X Linkage
As an example, weâ€™ll consider the inheritance of red green color blindness, which is X Linked.
The mutant allele is designated X rg.
Wild type alleles are designated X plus.
This designation reminds us that the rg allele is on the X chromosome.
We begin by mating a homozygous wild type female with a Color Blind Male.
The cross is written X plus X plus with X rg Y.
All F1 daughters are unaffected carriers, X plus X rg.
None of the F1 sons inherit the mutation because they received the Y chromosome, which lacks the gene.
One half of the F2 grandsons will be affected, X rg Y, the other half do not inherit the mutation, X plus Y.
None of the F2 granddaughters will be affected.
One half will be unaffected carriers, X plus X rg, the other half will not inherit the mutation, X plus X plus.
The distinguishing characteristic of X linkage is inheritance from affected grandfather, through unaffected carrier mother, to affected son.
X Inactivation
The Y chromosome has very few genes, the X chromosome has many.
Males have one X chromosome, females have two, so females have twice as many X Linked alleles as males.
This type of dosage imbalance cannot be tolerated in animals.
An example of this is Down syndrome, trisomy 21, which have three copies of every gene on chromosome 21 instead of the normal two.
To compensate, mammal females inactivate one or the other X in every cell of their bodies.
X inactivation occurs during early embryogenesis.
By the blastocyst stage one or the other X chromosome is inactivated in every blastomere.
Barr Bodies
X chromosomes are inactivated when they condense into heterochromatic masses known as Barr bodies. These can be observed by light microscopy.
The blastomeres proliferate into clusters of daughter cells. The same X chromosome is inactivated in all the cells of a cluster because they are all derived from the same blastomere.
In the ectoderm, these clusters develop into patches of skin, where the same X chromosome is inactivated in all the cells of a patch.
Thus, mammalian females are genetic mosaics, with a mixture of cells with one or the other inactivated X chromosome.
X inactivation is also called lyonization, after Mary Lyon who discovered it.
Calico Cats
Mosaic X inactivation can be observed with calico cats, where the orange hair grows from patches of epidermis with one inactivated X chromosome, the dark hair grows from epidermis with the other inactivated X.
Mosaic Ladies
Mosaic X inactivation was demonstrated in human females who were heterozygous for the X Linked disorder, anhidrotic ectodermal dysplasia (A E D).
One symptom of this disease is diminished sweat gland secretion, which lowers dermal electroconductivity.
Patches of epidermis with different electroconductivity was observed in heterozygous women.
All the cells of patches with low electroconductivity expressed the mutant A E D allele. The wild type allele was inactivated in these cells. Cells with high electroconductivity expressed the wild type allele and inactivated the mutant allele.
